## Curation, not capture

Orbital Artifacts doesn't operate satellites. The work is slower and
more ambivalent than that. Every print in this catalogue started as an
image in someone else's archive — specifically, the U.S. Geological
Survey's
[Earth as Art](https://eros.usgs.gov/media-gallery/earth-as-art) series,
a public-domain collection of Landsat, Terra, and ASTER views curated
by EROS Center scientists over two decades.

What the studio does is choose. What catches the eye. What sits next to
what. Which sequence of forty images, pulled from the six hundred and
fifty in the archive, tells a coherent story. Which ones earn a print.

If that sounds like a small job, try it some afternoon. The archive is
beautiful and overwhelming in the way only a public-good dataset can be
— there's no algorithm ranking any of it, no "trending," no editorial
line. Just image after image, each one a real place on a real day,
waiting for someone to decide it matters.

## What I look for

Not every place photographs well from 700 km up.

The easy scenes — deserts that happen to look like abstract paintings,
coastlines rendered in improbable blue — are everywhere in the Earth as
Art archive, and most of them are already famous. Those aren't what I'm
picking for.

The ones I keep are harder to explain: a scene where the colors feel
*earned*, where the geometry of a river delta or the grain of a dune
field carries some quiet weight that doesn't resolve into a single
pretty idea. Often they're places I've read about; sometimes they're
places I couldn't find on a map before and can't unsee after. The
catalogue number on a print is just the order I picked them in.

## False-color, explained

Most of the Earth as Art images are
[false-color composites](https://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/features/FalseColor).
That word *false* is misleading — the colors aren't invented, they're
*translated*. Satellites see more wavelengths than your eyes do; when
USGS scientists map invisible wavelengths (shortwave infrared, thermal)
onto the visible spectrum, you see exactly what's there, just with the
dial turned past red.

That's why healthy vegetation sometimes shows up scarlet, or why an
ostensible desert can bloom in turquoise. The colors mean something.
They're the best possible answer to the question *"what would this
place look like if my eyes were tuned slightly differently?"*

Each scene's sensor and acquisition date are listed on its page. When
USGS has published the specific band combination, it's there too.

## Sequencing

A catalogue isn't a playlist but it rhymes with one. OA-010 leads
because OA-010 is a good opening move — legible at a glance, slightly
strange on a second look, ready to set a tone. Every number after that
has to earn its place by contrast, adjacency, or repetition.

Some scenes are grouped because they share a sensor and a decade.
Others because they share a shape — an estuary's fingerprint echoing a
salt pan's; a volcanic caldera echoing an impact crater on the far side
of the world. The cataloguing is the art, or as much of it as this
studio claims.

## The frame

The last move is the one that turns a scientific composite into an
artifact: the frame.

Every print carries its own coordinates — latitude and longitude in
degrees, printed in a restrained monospaced font at the lower-right of
the mat. A tiny catalogue number sits at the lower-left, same as a
gallery label. The idea is simple: this is a real place on a real day,
and the frame tells you which one.

No one needs the metadata to enjoy the image. But for the people who
*do* look — who open a map and drop a pin, or who recognize their
hometown from the shape of a river bend — that restraint rewards them.

## Attribution, and a note on future work

The imagery belongs to the public. Landsat data is in the U.S. public
domain by statute; Copernicus Sentinel data carries a "free, full and
open" license. The USGS Earth as Art series is produced by federal
scientists as part of their official duties and released under the
same public-domain terms. The studio credits the source on every scene
page and in the footer of this site.

What *is* the studio's — and what a buyer is paying for — is the edit:
the selection, the sequencing, the catalogue, the frame, the paper,
the ink. A curator's authorship, not a photographer's.

Originally-processed work, made from raw scene data in the studio
rather than curated from the USGS archive, is planned but not yet on
this catalogue. When those scenes ship, they'll carry the studio's
own band choices and a separate
<a href="/process#processing">processing note</a>. Until then, every
print here is a curated edit — small task, slow work.

## The print

The files are printed on archival matte cotton paper, 320gsm, with
pigment inks rated for over a century of light resistance. Available
sizes and frame options vary by scene; see each scene's page for what's
in stock, or <a href="/contact">commission one</a>.

Orbital Artifacts ships worldwide from Sri Lanka.
